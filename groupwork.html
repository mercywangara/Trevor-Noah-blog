About The Book

The Handmaid's Tale by Margaret Atwood is a story about a woman named Offred in a scary future where a strict religious group called Gilead rules America. Women have no freedom, and Offred is a Handmaid, forced to have babies for a powerful man, the Commander, and his wife, Serena Joy. She lives under tight control, always watched, and remembers her old life with her husband and daughter, who were taken away.

PATRIACHY

Patriarchy is a social system built upon unequal power relations that men maintain over women.
In “The Handmaid’s Tale”, patriarchy is a theme that illustrates Gilead’s social structure, the
oppressive control men exert over women, and how it devalues women of their reproductive roles,
individuality, and basic rights. Men held all political, social, and economic power, while women were
categorized by their roles, eg, wives, marthas, and handmaids, each with a specific role. The handmaids,
for instance, were reduced to their ability to bear children and were seen as instruments of procreation,
while the wives were valued for their obedience to their husbands and the ability to run their households.
The novel also highlights religious patriarchy in the sense that roles for men and women were shaped
around biblical ideas of gender. For instance, in the bible where a slave or servant would perform the role
of a concubine, with the resulting children being considered as part of another woman. In Gilead, women
are stripped of their economic, political, and reproductive rights which aligns to the situations in our society
where women face discrimination in employment, limited access to education and restrictions in their bodily
rights for example, child marriages, forced marriages and lack of access to abortion services are real life
manifestations of control over women’s lives.

FEAR

In the book ‘The Handmaid’s Tale’, the concept of fear is exposed in many occasions amongst the young
women, who were only for the role of surrogacy, within the oppressive society of Gilead. Today, even in the
contrast of societal settings between Gilead and current society, the concept of fear is still portrayed in many
aspects. The Gilead setting was that, in any case of deviation from expected moral conduct, such as trying to
escape from designated bounds and committing adultery, heavy punishments, which are: breaking of legs and
being hung publicly respectively for the above cases, were executed on the victims, not only to stop them from
messing up again but also to send out a warning to the rest, who seemed to be lucky not to be in that situation
for the moment. Current society reveals fear in almost similar circumstances of unfair treatment yet choosing
to move on in silence. For example the fear to lose one’s job because of complaints to unfair work conditions,
the fear of imprisonment because of criticism to prevailing authorities and this can even include death cases
and cases of abduction, fear of religious leaders who tend to threaten their audiences with disastrous
experiences for violation of any instructions, among other fears.
